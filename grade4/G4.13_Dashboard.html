Strong Test with Good Low Guided Coverage

This assessment is one of the stronger G4 tests evaluated. Only 3 of 22 items (14%) are Scripted recall, well below the problematic levels seen on many assessments. The vast majority of questions (73%) operate at Low Guided level, and 3 items reach High Guided (Q9, Q12, Q13). Average complexity of 2.74 approaches the 3.0 target. The primary gap is the absence of any Doing Science items and the need to push more questions to High Guided.

What Do the Complexity Levels Mean?

From Achieve's Framework to Evaluate the Cognitive Complexity of Science Assessment Tasks (2019). Each question is classified based on how students are expected to think.

Scripted (Recall)
Student recalls a fact, definition, or formula. No reasoning required. Example: "What is the function of a root?" These are DOK Level 1 tasks. Achieve says NGSS-aligned tests should have 0% of these.
Low Guided (Application)
Student applies a known concept to a familiar scenario. Some reasoning, but the path is clear. Example: "A cart is heavy. Which action makes it easier to move?" Target: 40-50% of assessment.
High Guided (Transfer)
Student applies understanding to a new or unfamiliar situation. Must integrate multiple ideas and make sense of data or evidence. Example: "Given this data table, which claim about magnet strength is best supported?" Target: 20-30%.
Doing Science (Authentic)
Student engages in genuine scientific reasoning — analyzing novel data, constructing explanations, designing investigations. No single "right path." Example: "A population of mice changed color over 10 years. Use the evidence to explain why." Target: 5-10%.

Indicator Scores (Average Across All 22 Questions)

Achieve Cognitive Complexity Framework — Scale: 1 (Low) to 5 (High). Target for balanced assessment: ~3.0
What Are These Four Indicators?

Achieve's Cognitive Complexity Framework scores every assessment question on four dimensions. Together, these four scores determine the holistic classification (Scripted → Doing Science). A score of 1 means no engagement; 5 means deep, authentic engagement.

Scenario (Score: 2.9 avg)
Does the question present a phenomenon — something puzzling, observable, or real-world — that the student needs to figure out? A score of 1 means no context at all ("Define photosynthesis"). A score of 5 means a rich, novel scenario the student hasn't seen before.
SEP — Science & Engineering Practices (Score: 2.8 avg)
Does the question require the student to do something a scientist would do — analyze data, construct an explanation, argue from evidence, plan an investigation? A score of 1 means pure recall. A score of 5 means the student independently selects and applies a practice to solve a problem.
DCI — Disciplinary Core Ideas (Score: 2.8 avg)
How deeply does the question engage the science content? A score of 1 means recalling a definition. A score of 5 means applying core ideas to a new situation and connecting them across topics. For example: not just "What is a food chain?" but "Use this food web to predict what happens when one species is removed."
CCC — Crosscutting Concepts (Score: 2.6 avg)
Does the question require the student to use a big-picture thinking lens like Cause & Effect, Patterns, Structure & Function, Systems, or Scale? These are the transferable thinking tools NGSS says students need across all science domains. A score of 1 means no CCC is engaged at all. This is G4.13's weakest indicator, though stronger than many G3 assessments.

Content Coverage Flags

Issues with construct coverage and complexity distribution
  • Low Guided Ceiling for Most Questions
    16 of 22 questions (73%) cluster at Low Guided. While far better than Scripted-heavy tests, this creates a ceiling effect where most items require moderate reasoning but few demand the synthesis, evaluation, or novel application expected at High Guided or Doing Science levels.
  • Energy Conversion Cluster — 5 questions (Q14, Q15, Q16, Q17, Q18)
    Five PS questions all address energy topics (PS3-1 and PS3-2). Q16 and Q18 are both Scripted and test simple energy conversion identification. Consider replacing one with a higher-complexity energy question or shifting to an under-represented PS standard (PS4-1 waves).
  • LS Over-Represented: 23% vs. 12% Target
    Five LS questions (Q2-Q6) exceed the G4 target of 12%. Q2 (animal classification) is Scripted recall. Removing Q2 and reallocating to PS would improve domain balance, as PS is 9% below target.
  • No Doing Science Items
    The test lacks any questions at the highest complexity level. Achieve targets 5-10% of items at this level. The three High Guided items (Q9, Q12, Q13) show the test has the potential to reach this level — several Low Guided items could be elevated with multi-part designs and novel data.
  • Strong ETS Items Are a Highlight
    Q12 and Q13 are High Guided engineering design problems with explicit constraints and trade-off analysis. Q22 is a solid Low Guided ETS item. These represent the best question design on the test and should serve as models for redesigning weaker items.

Achieve Assessment Criteria Summary

Criteria for Procuring and Evaluating High-Quality Science Assessments (2018)
What Do These Criteria Measure?

Achieve published 7 criteria for evaluating science assessments. The 4 most relevant to individual question quality are shown below. Each question is rated: Meets, Partially Meets, or Does Not Meet.

Criterion 2: Three-Dimensional
Does the question require students to use all three NGSS dimensions together — a Science/Engineering Practice (SEP), a Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and a Crosscutting Concept (CCC)? A question that only tests vocabulary is not three-dimensional.
Criterion 3: Phenomena / Problems
Is the question anchored in a real-world phenomenon (something observable that needs explaining) or an engineering problem? Students should be figuring out why or how something happens, not just recalling a definition.
Criterion 5: Cognitive Complexity
Does the question require real thinking? It should go beyond recall (DOK1) and ask students to analyze, evaluate, or construct explanations. Achieve's standard: an NGSS-aligned assessment should have 0% pure recall items.
Criterion 6: Technical Quality
Is the question well-constructed? Scientifically accurate content, clear wording, plausible distractors (wrong answers that reveal real misconceptions), no cueing or trick phrasing. A question can be technically sound but still too simple.

Technically Excellent — Complexity Is the Growth Edge

Every question on G4.13 meets Criterion 6 for technical quality — accurate science, plausible distractors, and clear wording. Eight questions fully meet the phenomena criterion, and 18 of 22 partially meet three-dimensional standards. The primary growth area is pushing more items beyond Low Guided to High Guided and Doing Science levels, especially by adding multi-part questions and novel data analysis tasks.

Redesign Q2 (animal classification recall) into evidence-based reasoning
Q2 asks students to match trait descriptions to animal groups — pure recall classification. Replace with a question that presents unfamiliar organism trait data and asks students to explain how structures help survival.
Current (Scripted)
"Which pair of animals has scales and controls body temperature?"
→
Redesigned (High Guided)
"A scientist discovers a new wetland animal with webbed feet, a flat tail, and thick oily fur. Based on these structures, explain how this animal is adapted to life in water. Which known animal group does it most resemble, and what additional feature would help confirm your classification?"
2
Redesign Q16 (toy car energy) and Q18 (hand rubbing) — both obvious energy identification
Q16 asks which observation shows electrical-to-motion conversion (answer: the car moves — too obvious). Q18 asks which energy converts to heat when rubbing hands (answer: motion — simple recall). Both should require students to trace energy through multiple conversions with data.
Current (Scripted)
"Which observation shows electrical energy is being converted to motion energy?"
→
Redesigned (High Guided)
"A battery-powered car runs for 10 minutes. Students measure: the car travels 50 meters, the motor gets warm, and the headlights dim over time. Create an energy flow diagram showing ALL the energy conversions happening. Why do the headlights dim — what does this tell you about energy in the system?"

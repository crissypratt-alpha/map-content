Achieve Standard: 0% DOK1 Items

Achieve's criteria state that high-quality NGSS-aligned assessments should contain 0% DOK Level 1 (recall) tasks. On this assessment, an estimated 50% of items are at DOK1 (all 11 Scripted items). While this test is slightly better than the worst G3 assessments, half the questions still require only recall. The one Doing Science item (Q14 — toy car energy/speed) and one High Guided item (Q13 — flood protection constraints) show what the rest of the test could aspire to.

What Do the Complexity Levels Mean?

From Achieve's Framework to Evaluate the Cognitive Complexity of Science Assessment Tasks (2019). Each question is classified based on how students are expected to think.

Scripted (Recall)
Student recalls a fact, definition, or formula. No reasoning required. Example: "What type of energy does a solar cell produce?" These are DOK Level 1 tasks. Achieve says NGSS-aligned tests should have 0% of these.
Low Guided (Application)
Student applies a known concept to a familiar scenario. Some reasoning, but the path is clear. Example: "Ice is melting on a car hood after the engine runs. Why?" Target: 40-50% of assessment.
High Guided (Transfer)
Student applies understanding to a new or unfamiliar situation. Must integrate multiple ideas and make sense of data or evidence. Example: "A community center needs flood protection with 4 constraints. Which solution meets ALL requirements?" Target: 20-30%.
Doing Science (Authentic)
Student engages in genuine scientific reasoning — analyzing novel data, constructing explanations, designing investigations. No single "right path." Example: "Three toy cars have equal mass but different speeds. Use the data to explain the energy relationship." Target: 5-10%.

Achieve Cognitive Complexity Framework — Scale: 1 (Low) to 5 (High). Target for balanced assessment: ~3.0
What Are These Four Indicators?

Achieve's Cognitive Complexity Framework scores every assessment question on four dimensions. Together, these four scores determine the holistic classification (Scripted → Doing Science). A score of 1 means no engagement; 5 means deep, authentic engagement.

Scenario (Score: 2.4 avg)
Does the question present a phenomenon — something puzzling, observable, or real-world — that the student needs to figure out? A score of 1 means no context at all ("What energy does a solar cell produce?"). A score of 5 means a rich, novel scenario the student hasn't seen before.
SEP — Science & Engineering Practices (Score: 2.2 avg)
Does the question require the student to do something a scientist would do — analyze data, construct an explanation, argue from evidence, plan an investigation? A score of 1 means pure recall. A score of 5 means the student independently selects and applies a practice to solve a problem.
DCI — Disciplinary Core Ideas (Score: 2.4 avg)
How deeply does the question engage the science content? A score of 1 means recalling a definition. A score of 5 means applying core ideas to a new situation and connecting them across topics. For example: not just "What does a switch do?" but "Given this circuit with three switches, predict which combination lights the bulb."
CCC — Crosscutting Concepts (Score: 2.0 avg)
Does the question require the student to use a big-picture thinking lens like Cause & Effect, Patterns, Energy & Matter, Systems, or Scale? These are the transferable thinking tools NGSS says students need across all science domains. A score of 1 means no CCC is engaged at all. This is G4.16's weakest area.

Content Coverage Flags

Issues with construct redundancy and topic balance
  • CRITICAL: Energy Conversion Recall — 4 questions (Q16, Q17, Q18, Q19)
    Four consecutive questions test essentially the same construct: "Device X converts energy type A to energy type B." Q17 (solar cell), Q18 (stove), and Q19 (circuit to sound) are all Scripted recall of single-step energy conversions. Q16 (circuit switch) is closely related. 18% of the test assesses the same narrow skill at the lowest complexity level. Remove 2-3 and replace with diverse energy transfer or systems questions.
  • Duplicate: Topographic Map Interpretation — 2 questions (Q10, Q11)
    Q10 applies contour line reading to an actual map (Low Guided). Q11 asks the same concept as pure declarative recall (Scripted). Q11 is entirely redundant. Remove Q11.
  • Overlap: LS1-1 Body Systems Cluster — 3 questions (Q3, Q4, Q5)
    Three questions test body system knowledge under the same standard. While they target different systems (respiratory/circulatory, digestive, adaptation), all are at the same Low Guided level. Consider replacing one with a question that integrates multiple systems.
  • Engineering Under-Represented
    Only 2 ETS questions (Q12, Q13) — 9% vs. the 18% target. Q12 is pure recall about hurricane protection purpose. The freed slot from redundancy removal should add an applied ETS question requiring iterative design evaluation.
  • Bright Spot: Q13 and Q14 Show What This Test Could Be
    Q13 (flood protection with 4 constraints) is the only High Guided item. Q14 (toy car speed/energy with data) is the only Doing Science item. These two questions demonstrate that the test writers can produce rigorous items — but 20 of 22 questions fall below this standard.
  • PS3-2 Overload
    Five questions target the same standard (4-PS3-2: energy conversion/transfer) — Q15, Q16, Q17, Q18, Q19. This is 23% of the test on a single performance expectation. Achieve recommends broad PE coverage across a grade band.

Achieve Assessment Criteria Summary

Criteria for Procuring and Evaluating High-Quality Science Assessments (2018)
What Do These Criteria Measure?

Achieve published 7 criteria for evaluating science assessments. The 4 most relevant to individual question quality are shown below. Each question is rated: Meets, Partially Meets, or Does Not Meet.

Criterion 2: Three-Dimensional
Does the question require students to use all three NGSS dimensions together — a Science/Engineering Practice (SEP), a Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and a Crosscutting Concept (CCC)? A question that only tests vocabulary is not three-dimensional.
Criterion 3: Phenomena / Problems
Is the question anchored in a real-world phenomenon (something observable that needs explaining) or an engineering problem? Students should be figuring out why or how something happens, not just recalling a definition.
Criterion 5: Cognitive Complexity
Does the question require real thinking? It should go beyond recall (DOK1) and ask students to analyze, evaluate, or construct explanations. Achieve's standard: an NGSS-aligned assessment should have 0% pure recall items.
Criterion 6: Technical Quality
Is the question well-constructed? Scientifically accurate content, clear wording, plausible distractors (wrong answers that reveal real misconceptions), no cueing or trick phrasing. A question can be technically sound but still too simple.

Technically Sound but Cognitively Shallow — With Two Notable Exceptions

Most questions are well-constructed with accurate science and plausible distractors. The fundamental problem is not question quality — it's that they test the wrong cognitive level. However, Q13 (flood protection constraints) and Q14 (toy car energy data analysis) prove this test can produce genuinely rigorous items. The remaining 20 questions need to be elevated to match the standard set by these two.

Achieve Standard: 0% DOK1 Items

Achieve's criteria state that high-quality NGSS-aligned assessments should contain 0% DOK Level 1 (recall) tasks. On this assessment, an estimated 53% of items are at DOK1 (all 16 Scripted items). While the test has a stronger upper half than most G3 assessments — including one High Guided question (Q14) and 13 Low Guided items — over half the questions still rely on recall. The average complexity of 2.24 falls well below the ~3.0 target for a balanced assessment.

What Do the Complexity Levels Mean?

From Achieve's Framework to Evaluate the Cognitive Complexity of Science Assessment Tasks (2019). Each question is classified based on how students are expected to think.

Scripted (Recall)
Student recalls a fact, definition, or formula. No reasoning required. Example: "What is the function of a root?" These are DOK Level 1 tasks. Achieve says NGSS-aligned tests should have 0% of these.
Low Guided (Application)
Student applies a known concept to a familiar scenario. Some reasoning, but the path is clear. Example: "A cart is heavy. Which action makes it easier to move?" Target: 40-50% of assessment.
High Guided (Transfer)
Student applies understanding to a new or unfamiliar situation. Must integrate multiple ideas and make sense of data or evidence. Example: "Given this data table, which claim about magnet strength is best supported?" Target: 20-30%.
Doing Science (Authentic)
Student engages in genuine scientific reasoning — analyzing novel data, constructing explanations, designing investigations. No single "right path." Example: "A population of mice changed color over 10 years. Use the evidence to explain why." Target: 5-10%.

Indicator Scores (Average Across All 30 Questions)

Achieve Cognitive Complexity Framework — Scale: 1 (Low) to 5 (High). Target for balanced assessment: ~3.0
What Are These Four Indicators?

Achieve's Cognitive Complexity Framework scores every assessment question on four dimensions. Together, these four scores determine the holistic classification (Scripted → Doing Science). A score of 1 means no engagement; 5 means deep, authentic engagement.

Scenario (Score: 2.5 avg)
Does the question present a phenomenon — something puzzling, observable, or real-world — that the student needs to figure out? A score of 1 means no context at all ("Define photosynthesis"). A score of 5 means a rich, novel scenario the student hasn't seen before.
SEP — Science & Engineering Practices (Score: 2.0 avg)
Does the question require the student to do something a scientist would do — analyze data, construct an explanation, argue from evidence, plan an investigation? A score of 1 means pure recall. A score of 5 means the student independently selects and applies a practice to solve a problem.
DCI — Disciplinary Core Ideas (Score: 2.4 avg)
How deeply does the question engage the science content? A score of 1 means recalling a definition. A score of 5 means applying core ideas to a new situation and connecting them across topics. For example: not just "What is energy?" but "Use this data to trace the energy transfers in a collision."
CCC — Crosscutting Concepts (Score: 2.0 avg)
Does the question require the student to use a big-picture thinking lens like Cause & Effect, Energy & Matter, Patterns, Structure & Function, or Systems? These are the transferable thinking tools NGSS says students need across all science domains. A score of 1 means no CCC is engaged at all. Tied with SEP as G4.10's weakest area.

Content Coverage Flags

Issues with construct redundancy, topic balance, and image-dependent items
  • CRITICAL: Energy Transfer in Collisions — 2 identical-construct questions (Q6, Q7)
    Both test the same concept (energy transfers from a moving object to a stationary object during a collision) with nearly identical setups — Q6 uses marbles, Q7 uses toy cars. Both are Low Guided at 3.0 avg. Remove Q7, keep Q6.
  • Duplicate: Fossil Layer Interpretation — 2 questions (Q21, Q22)
    Both present a sequence of fossil layers and ask students to conclude how the environment changed over time. Q21 and Q22 are essentially the same construct with slightly different fossils. Remove Q22, keep Q21.
  • Duplicate: Erosion Recall — 2 questions (Q23, Q24)
    Both test recall-level identification of erosion processes and the landforms they create. Q23 asks the process name; Q24 asks the landform name. Both are Scripted. Combine into one application-level question or remove one.
  • Cluster: Energy Conversion — 4 questions on PS3-2 (Q3, Q5, Q8, Q9)
    Four questions address energy transformation or transfer. Q3 (freezing water) and Q5 (solar fan) are solid Low Guided items. Q8 (toaster conversion) is pure recall. Q9 (circuit conductor) is borderline. Remove Q8, keep Q3 and Q5, redesign Q9 with data.
  • Cluster: Sensory/Body Systems — 3 questions on LS1-2 (Q18, Q19, Q20)
    Q19 is pure nervous system recall despite a scenario wrapper. Q18 and Q20 both test environmental signals and sensory classification at Scripted level. Consolidate to one well-designed question that requires systems-level reasoning.
  • 5 Image-Dependent Questions Could Not Be Fully Evaluated (Q26-30)
    Questions 26-30 rely heavily on images for their content and could not be fully assessed. These were conservatively rated as Scripted. Their actual complexity may differ. Original test images should be reviewed to complete these evaluations.
  • Engineering Severely Under-Represented
    Only 2 ETS questions (7%) vs. the NGSS target of 18%. Both image-dependent questions (Q29, Q30) could not be fully evaluated. The test needs at least 3-4 more ETS questions addressing design thinking and problem-solving.
  • Only 1 Question Reaches High Guided (Q14)
    The lost camper signaling question is the only item requiring genuine transfer-level reasoning. Achieve targets 25-40% of questions at High Guided or Doing Science. G4.10 is at 3%.

Achieve Assessment Criteria Summary

Criteria for Procuring and Evaluating High-Quality Science Assessments (2018)
What Do These Criteria Measure?

Achieve published 7 criteria for evaluating science assessments. The 4 most relevant to individual question quality are shown below. Each question is rated: Meets, Partially Meets, or Does Not Meet.

Criterion 2: Three-Dimensional
Does the question require students to use all three NGSS dimensions together — a Science/Engineering Practice (SEP), a Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and a Crosscutting Concept (CCC)? A question that only tests vocabulary is not three-dimensional.
Criterion 3: Phenomena / Problems
Is the question anchored in a real-world phenomenon (something observable that needs explaining) or an engineering problem? Students should be figuring out why or how something happens, not just recalling a definition.
Criterion 5: Cognitive Complexity
Does the question require real thinking? It should go beyond recall (DOK1) and ask students to analyze, evaluate, or construct explanations. Achieve's standard: an NGSS-aligned assessment should have 0% pure recall items.
Criterion 6: Technical Quality
Is the question well-constructed? Scientifically accurate content, clear wording, plausible distractors (wrong answers that reveal real misconceptions), no cueing or trick phrasing. A question can be technically sound but still too simple.

The test also has more scenario-grounded questions than typical G3 assessments — 18 partially meet Criterion 3 for phenomena. However, no question fully meets three-dimensional or complexity criteria. The fundamental gap remains: questions test recognition and classification rather than reasoning, analysis, and explanation. Q14 (lost camper) shows what the upper range could look like.

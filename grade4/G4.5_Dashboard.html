Achieve Standard: 0% DOK1 Items

Achieve's criteria state that high-quality NGSS-aligned assessments should contain 0% DOK Level 1 (recall) tasks. On this assessment, an estimated 94% of items are at DOK1 (all 30 Scripted items). With an average complexity of just 1.52, this is one of the weakest assessments evaluated. Multiple questions test below grade level (Q17, Q19, Q20, Q30, Q31), asking content appropriate for Grades K-2 rather than Grade 4.

What Do the Complexity Levels Mean?

From Achieve's Framework to Evaluate the Cognitive Complexity of Science Assessment Tasks (2019). Each question is classified based on how students are expected to think.

Scripted (Recall)
Student recalls a fact, definition, or formula. No reasoning required. Example: "What is the function of a root?" These are DOK Level 1 tasks. Achieve says NGSS-aligned tests should have 0% of these.
Low Guided (Application)
Student applies a known concept to a familiar scenario. Some reasoning, but the path is clear. Example: "A cart is heavy. Which action makes it easier to move?" Target: 40-50% of assessment.
High Guided (Transfer)
Student applies understanding to a new or unfamiliar situation. Must integrate multiple ideas and make sense of data or evidence. Example: "Given this data table, which claim about magnet strength is best supported?" Target: 20-30%.
Doing Science (Authentic)
Student engages in genuine scientific reasoning — analyzing novel data, constructing explanations, designing investigations. No single "right path." Example: "A population of mice changed color over 10 years. Use the evidence to explain why." Target: 5-10%.

Achieve Cognitive Complexity Framework — Scale: 1 (Low) to 5 (High). Target for balanced assessment: ~3.0
What Are These Four Indicators?

Achieve's Cognitive Complexity Framework scores every assessment question on four dimensions. Together, these four scores determine the holistic classification (Scripted → Doing Science). A score of 1 means no engagement; 5 means deep, authentic engagement.

Scenario (Score: 1.6 avg)
Does the question present a phenomenon — something puzzling, observable, or real-world — that the student needs to figure out? A score of 1 means no context at all ("Define photosynthesis"). A score of 5 means a rich, novel scenario the student hasn't seen before.
SEP — Science & Engineering Practices (Score: 1.3 avg)
Does the question require the student to do something a scientist would do — analyze data, construct an explanation, argue from evidence, plan an investigation? A score of 1 means pure recall. A score of 5 means the student independently selects and applies a practice to solve a problem.
DCI — Disciplinary Core Ideas (Score: 1.9 avg)
How deeply does the question engage the science content? A score of 1 means recalling a definition. A score of 5 means applying core ideas to a new situation and connecting them across topics. For example: not just "What is a food chain?" but "Use this food web to predict what happens when one species is removed."
CCC — Crosscutting Concepts (Score: 1.3 avg)
Does the question require the student to use a big-picture thinking lens like Cause & Effect, Patterns, Structure & Function, Systems, or Scale? These are the transferable thinking tools NGSS says students need across all science domains. A score of 1 means no CCC is engaged at all. This is G4.5's weakest area (tied with SEP).

Content Coverage Flags

Issues with construct redundancy, below-grade-level content, and topic balance
  • CRITICAL: Structure-Function Recall Overload — 6 questions (Q3, Q6, Q14, Q22, Q26, Q29)
    19% of the test asks identical "Which body part/structure allows [organism] to [function]?" at the same Scripted recall level. All six are pure anatomy identification with no reasoning about how structure enables function. Achieve recommends diverse construct coverage. Remove 4-5 and replace with under-represented standards (PS, ETS).
  • CRITICAL: Fossil Superposition — 3 questions (Q7, Q8, Q24)
    Three questions test the same simple superposition principle ("deeper = older" or "same depth = same age"). Q7 and Q8 are nearly identical in structure. Remove 2 of 3 and replace with higher-complexity ESS items.
  • Duplicate: Clean Energy Recall — 2 questions (Q1, Q23)
    Q1 asks about clean energy for air; Q23 asks about clean energy for water. Both are pure recall of energy source classification. Remove one, redesign the other to include emissions data for analysis.
  • Duplicate: Energy Transfer Identification — 3 questions (Q4, Q15, Q18)
    All three test energy conversion recall (car engine, household devices, sailboat). Q15 (TEI with three devices) has the most breadth. Remove Q4 and Q18, strengthen Q15.
  • Below Grade Level — 5 questions (Q17, Q19, Q20, Q30, Q31)
    These test concepts appropriate for Grades K-2: "What does a thermometer measure?" (Q20), "Which unit measures plant height?" (Q30), "Which body part detects smell?" (Q31), "Plants need water and sunlight" (Q19), "What tool measures freezing point?" (Q17). All scored 1.0 average complexity. These should not appear on a Grade 4 assessment.
  • Severe Domain Imbalance: Life Science Dominance
    Life Science has 10 questions (31%) despite an NGSS target of only 12%. Meanwhile, Physical Science is at 22% vs. a 41% target. Use freed slots from redundancy removal to add PS items covering waves and energy transfer at higher complexity.
  • No Questions Above Low Guided
    The highest complexity level on this test is Low Guided (Q5, Q21 only). Zero questions reach High Guided or Doing Science. Achieve targets 25-40% of questions at these upper levels. This is the most extreme gap across the entire assessment.

Achieve Assessment Criteria Summary

Criteria for Procuring and Evaluating High-Quality Science Assessments (2018)
What Do These Criteria Measure?

Achieve published 7 criteria for evaluating science assessments. The 4 most relevant to individual question quality are shown below. Each question is rated: Meets, Partially Meets, or Does Not Meet.

Criterion 2: Three-Dimensional
Does the question require students to use all three NGSS dimensions together — a Science/Engineering Practice (SEP), a Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and a Crosscutting Concept (CCC)? A question that only tests vocabulary is not three-dimensional.
Criterion 3: Phenomena / Problems
Is the question anchored in a real-world phenomenon (something observable that needs explaining) or an engineering problem? Students should be figuring out why or how something happens, not just recalling a definition.
Criterion 5: Cognitive Complexity
Does the question require real thinking? It should go beyond recall (DOK1) and ask students to analyze, evaluate, or construct explanations. Achieve's standard: an NGSS-aligned assessment should have 0% pure recall items.
Criterion 6: Technical Quality
Is the question well-constructed? Scientifically accurate content, clear wording, plausible distractors (wrong answers that reveal real misconceptions), no cueing or trick phrasing. A question can be technically sound but still too simple.

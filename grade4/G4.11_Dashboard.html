One of the Stronger G4 Tests — But Still Below Achieve Standards

With only 19% Scripted items and an average complexity of 2.58, G4.11 performs well above most assessments in the Grade 4 suite. 76% of questions reach Low Guided, meaning most items require application rather than pure recall. However, only 1 question (5%) reaches High Guided and none reach Doing Science. Achieve's criteria call for 0% DOK1 (recall) tasks and 25-40% of items at upper complexity levels. The test is strongest in Earth Science (erosion/topography) and circuits, but shows clear domain imbalance with 0% Engineering (ETS) coverage against an 18% target.

What Do the Complexity Levels Mean?

From Achieve's Framework to Evaluate the Cognitive Complexity of Science Assessment Tasks (2019). Each question is classified based on how students are expected to think.

Scripted (Recall)
Student recalls a fact, definition, or formula. No reasoning required. Example: "What is the function of a root?" These are DOK Level 1 tasks. Achieve says NGSS-aligned tests should have 0% of these.
Low Guided (Application)
Student applies a known concept to a familiar scenario. Some reasoning, but the path is clear. Example: "A cart is heavy. Which action makes it easier to move?" Target: 40-50% of assessment.
High Guided (Transfer)
Student applies understanding to a new or unfamiliar situation. Must integrate multiple ideas and make sense of data or evidence. Example: "Given this data table, which claim about magnet strength is best supported?" Target: 20-30%.
Doing Science (Authentic)
Student engages in genuine scientific reasoning — analyzing novel data, constructing explanations, designing investigations. No single "right path." Example: "A population of mice changed color over 10 years. Use the evidence to explain why." Target: 5-10%.

Achieve Cognitive Complexity Framework — Scale: 1 (Low) to 5 (High). Target for balanced assessment: ~3.0
What Are These Four Indicators?

Achieve's Cognitive Complexity Framework scores every assessment question on four dimensions. Together, these four scores determine the holistic classification (Scripted → Doing Science). A score of 1 means no engagement; 5 means deep, authentic engagement.

Scenario (Score: 2.5 avg)
Does the question present a phenomenon — something puzzling, observable, or real-world — that the student needs to figure out? A score of 1 means no context at all ("Define photosynthesis"). A score of 5 means a rich, novel scenario the student hasn't seen before.
SEP — Science & Engineering Practices (Score: 2.6 avg)
Does the question require the student to do something a scientist would do — analyze data, construct an explanation, argue from evidence, plan an investigation? A score of 1 means pure recall. A score of 5 means the student independently selects and applies a practice to solve a problem.
DCI — Disciplinary Core Ideas (Score: 2.7 avg)
How deeply does the question engage the science content? A score of 1 means recalling a definition. A score of 5 means applying core ideas to a new situation and connecting them across topics. For example: not just "What is a food chain?" but "Use this food web to predict what happens when one species is removed."
CCC — Crosscutting Concepts (Score: 2.5 avg)
Does the question require the student to use a big-picture thinking lens like Cause & Effect, Patterns, Structure & Function, Systems, or Scale? These are the transferable thinking tools NGSS says students need across all science domains. A score of 1 means no CCC is engaged at all. This is G4.11's weakest area (tied with Scenario).

Content Coverage Flags

Issues with construct redundancy, topic balance, and answer key accuracy
  • CRITICAL: Answer Key Error — Q12 (Evaporation Energy Forms)
    Q12 asks which energy forms are compared in an evaporation investigation. The answer key gives "chemical and sound energy" (choice D), but evaporation is driven by light and thermal energy (choice C). This is a scientifically inaccurate answer key. Review and correct before administering.
  • Overrepresentation: Erosion & Weathering — 7 questions (Q1, Q5, Q9, Q10, Q14, Q19, Q20)
    33% of the test covers erosion, weathering, and Earth surface processes. While this is an important ESS topic, having 7 questions on closely related concepts crowds out other domains. Consider reducing to 4-5 questions and using freed slots for ETS coverage.
  • Overrepresentation: Circuits & Electrical Energy — 6 questions (Q2, Q7a, Q7b, Q8, Q11, Q18)
    29% of the test focuses on circuits. Q2, Q8, Q11, and Q18 all involve identifying components or connections in circuit diagrams. While Q7a/Q7b form a valuable linked pair and Q8 is a strong diagnostic task, Q18 is functionally similar to Q2 and Q11. Remove one.
  • Cluster: Topographic Map Reading — 3 questions (Q1, Q9, Q20)
    All three use topographic maps. While each asks something different (deposition, erosion, terrain description), having 3 of 21 questions on map reading may over-represent this narrow skill. Consider converting one to a different ESS data representation.
  • Zero ETS Coverage
    No questions address Engineering, Technology, and Applications of Science. The G4 NGSS target is 18% ETS. This is the single largest domain gap on this test. Use 3-4 freed question slots for engineering design process items (3-5-ETS1-1 through 3-5-ETS1-3).
  • Renewable Resources — Q3 and Q16 Both Test Classification Recall
    Both Q3 and Q16 test renewable vs. nonrenewable resource classification at the Scripted level. Q3 uses tables (slightly better format) while Q16 is pure recall with no context. Remove Q16 and redesign Q3 around an energy planning scenario.

Achieve Assessment Criteria Summary

Criteria for Procuring and Evaluating High-Quality Science Assessments (2018)
What Do These Criteria Measure?

Achieve published 7 criteria for evaluating science assessments. The 4 most relevant to individual question quality are shown below. Each question is rated: Meets, Partially Meets, or Does Not Meet.

Criterion 2: Three-Dimensional
Does the question require students to use all three NGSS dimensions together — a Science/Engineering Practice (SEP), a Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and a Crosscutting Concept (CCC)? A question that only tests vocabulary is not three-dimensional.
Criterion 3: Phenomena / Problems
Is the question anchored in a real-world phenomenon (something observable that needs explaining) or an engineering problem? Students should be figuring out why or how something happens, not just recalling a definition.
Criterion 5: Cognitive Complexity
Does the question require real thinking? It should go beyond recall (DOK1) and ask students to analyze, evaluate, or construct explanations. Achieve's standard: an NGSS-aligned assessment should have 0% pure recall items.
Criterion 6: Technical Quality
Is the question well-constructed? Scientifically accurate content, clear wording, plausible distractors (wrong answers that reveal real misconceptions), no cueing or trick phrasing. A question can be technically sound but still too simple.

Stronger Than Most G4 Tests: Partially Meeting Criteria at High Rates

G4.11 stands out because 76% of questions partially meet three-dimensionality — most items engage at least two NGSS dimensions. Technical quality is excellent (86% meet). The primary gap is that no questions fully meet Criteria 2, 3, or 5: items engage phenomena and practices at an application level but do not push to authentic, student-driven reasoning. Elevating the strongest Low Guided items to High Guided would move several criteria ratings from "Partially Meets" to "Meets."

Priority 3: Fill the Engineering (ETS) Gap
6
Add 3-4 ETS questions using freed slots
G4.11 has 0% ETS against an 18% target. After removing Q16 and reducing erosion/circuit redundancy, use freed slots for:
  • ETS1-1: Define a problem — students analyze erosion data from a schoolyard and identify what needs to be solved

These ETS items can integrate with the test's existing ESS and PS content, maintaining thematic coherence while adding the missing dimension.

Add reasoning prompts that make the CCC visible: "What pattern do you notice in the data?", "What caused the change?", "How does this structure help the organism?" Only Q15 (score: 4) currently requires explicit CCC reasoning — this should be the model for all items.
Biggest opportunity for across-the-board improvement

Ask students to reason about rates of change, thresholds, and long-term vs. short-term effects. Example: "How would the canyon change if rainfall doubled? Would the change be fast or slow?

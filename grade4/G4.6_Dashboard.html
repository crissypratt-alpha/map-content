Weakest of All G4 Tests — Heavily Image-Based, Major Content Misalignment

Achieve's criteria state that high-quality NGSS-aligned assessments should contain 0% DOK Level 1 (recall) tasks. On this assessment, an estimated 81% of items are at DOK1 (all 26 Scripted items). Additionally, 16 of 32 questions (50%) have stems embedded entirely in images that could not be extracted for evaluation, and at least 14 questions test Grade 5 NGSS content (food webs, symbiotic relationships, Earth/Moon/Sun system) rather than Grade 4 standards. This is the weakest G4 assessment evaluated.

What Do the Complexity Levels Mean?

From Achieve's Framework to Evaluate the Cognitive Complexity of Science Assessment Tasks (2019). Each question is classified based on how students are expected to think.

Scripted (Recall)
Student recalls a fact, definition, or formula. No reasoning required. Example: "What is the function of a root?" These are DOK Level 1 tasks. Achieve says NGSS-aligned tests should have 0% of these.
Low Guided (Application)
Student applies a known concept to a familiar scenario. Some reasoning, but the path is clear. Example: "A cart is heavy. Which action makes it easier to move?" Target: 40-50% of assessment.
High Guided (Transfer)
Student applies understanding to a new or unfamiliar situation. Must integrate multiple ideas and make sense of data or evidence. Example: "Given this data table, which claim about magnet strength is best supported?" Target: 20-30%.
Doing Science (Authentic)
Student engages in genuine scientific reasoning — analyzing novel data, constructing explanations, designing investigations. No single "right path." Example: "A population of mice changed color over 10 years. Use the evidence to explain why." Target: 5-10%.

Indicator Scores (Average Across All 32 Questions)

Achieve Cognitive Complexity Framework — Scale: 1 (Low) to 5 (High). Target for balanced assessment: ~3.0
What Are These Four Indicators?

Achieve's Cognitive Complexity Framework scores every assessment question on four dimensions. Together, these four scores determine the holistic classification (Scripted → Doing Science). A score of 1 means no engagement; 5 means deep, authentic engagement.

Scenario (Score: 1.47 avg)
Does the question present a phenomenon — something puzzling, observable, or real-world — that the student needs to figure out? A score of 1 means no context at all ("Define photosynthesis"). A score of 5 means a rich, novel scenario the student hasn't seen before. Half the questions on G4.6 could not be evaluated because stems were embedded in images.
SEP — Science & Engineering Practices (Score: 1.31 avg)
Does the question require the student to do something a scientist would do — analyze data, construct an explanation, argue from evidence, plan an investigation? A score of 1 means pure recall. A score of 5 means the student independently selects and applies a practice to solve a problem. G4.6's lowest evaluable indicator.
DCI — Disciplinary Core Ideas (Score: 1.44 avg)
How deeply does the question engage the science content? A score of 1 means recalling a definition. A score of 5 means applying core ideas to a new situation and connecting them across topics. Many G4.6 items test Grade 5 content (food webs, symbiotic relationships, Earth/Moon/Sun system) rather than Grade 4 DCIs.
CCC — Crosscutting Concepts (Score: 1.19 avg)
Does the question require the student to use a big-picture thinking lens like Cause & Effect, Patterns, Structure & Function, Systems, or Scale? These are the transferable thinking tools NGSS says students need across all science domains. A score of 1 means no CCC is engaged at all. This is G4.6's weakest indicator at 1.19.

Content Coverage Flags

Critical issues with grade alignment, image dependency, and content balance
  • CRITICAL: Grade 5 Content Misalignment — at least 14 questions (44%)
    Food webs, symbiotic relationships (mutualism, parasitism, predator-prey), and Earth/Moon/Sun system topics are all Grade 5 NGSS standards (5-LS2-1, 5-ESS1-2). Nearly half the test assesses content students should not encounter until the following year. This fundamentally undermines the assessment's validity as a Grade 4 measure.
  • CRITICAL: Image-Only Stems — 16 questions (50%) could not be fully evaluated
    Half of all question stems are embedded entirely in images with no text extraction possible. This prevents accurate cognitive complexity evaluation, creates accessibility barriers for screen readers, and means the true complexity distribution may differ from what is reported here. All 16 were conservatively scored at floor level (1.0).
  • Severe Redundancy: Food Web/Ecosystem Questions — 8-9 questions (~28%)
    Questions Q1, Q4, Q7, Q9, Q10, Q19, Q22a/b, Q25, Q26, and Q29 all test food web or organism relationship concepts. This represents massive over-testing of a single construct that is not even aligned to Grade 4 NGSS. Remove at least 6 of these and replace with Grade 4-aligned content.
  • Duplicate: Parasitism/Symbiosis — Q1 and Q4
    Both test organism relationship classification (Q1: oxpeckers on zebras, Q4: fleas on wolves). Same concept, same cognitive level. Remove Q4, which is simpler.
  • Severe Domain Imbalance
    Physical Science has only 1 identifiable question (3%) vs. the 41% NGSS target. Engineering/Technology has 0 questions vs. 18% target. Life Science is massively over-represented at 34% (target 12%). Even accounting for the 16 unknown items, the identifiable distribution is badly skewed toward off-grade LS content.
  • No Questions Above Low Guided
    The highest complexity level on this test is Low Guided (6 questions, 19%). Zero questions reach High Guided or Doing Science. Achieve targets 25-40% of questions at these upper levels. With an average complexity of 1.35, this is the lowest average of any G4 assessment.

Achieve Assessment Criteria Summary

Criteria for Procuring and Evaluating High-Quality Science Assessments (2018)
What Do These Criteria Measure?

Achieve published 7 criteria for evaluating science assessments. The 4 most relevant to individual question quality are shown below. Each question is rated: Meets, Partially Meets, or Does Not Meet.

Criterion 2: Three-Dimensional
Does the question require students to use all three NGSS dimensions together — a Science/Engineering Practice (SEP), a Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and a Crosscutting Concept (CCC)? A question that only tests vocabulary is not three-dimensional.
Criterion 3: Phenomena / Problems
Is the question anchored in a real-world phenomenon (something observable that needs explaining) or an engineering problem? Students should be figuring out why or how something happens, not just recalling a definition.
Criterion 5: Cognitive Complexity
Does the question require real thinking? It should go beyond recall (DOK1) and ask students to analyze, evaluate, or construct explanations. Achieve's standard: an NGSS-aligned assessment should have 0% pure recall items.
Criterion 6: Technical Quality
Is the question well-constructed? Scientifically accurate content, clear wording, plausible distractors (wrong answers that reveal real misconceptions), no cueing or trick phrasing. A question can be technically sound but still too simple.

Cannot Evaluate Half the Test — Image Dependency Obscures True Quality

22 of 32 questions received "Cannot Evaluate" for Technical Quality because their stems were embedded in images. Of the 10 questions that could be assessed, 3 meet and 7 partially meet technical quality criteria. The evaluable questions are technically adequate but cognitively shallow and frequently misaligned to Grade 4 NGSS. The image-dependency problem makes this assessment the hardest to evaluate in the entire G4 suite and means these results likely understate the true complexity level.

Replace classification tasks with evidence-based reasoning
The evaluable questions on G4.6 primarily ask students to classify or label (parasitism, food chain order, day/night cause). MAP never tests this way. Instead, MAP presents observation data and asks students to identify patterns, construct explanations, and support claims with evidence.

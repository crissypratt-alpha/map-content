Achieve Standard: 0% DOK1 Items

Achieve's criteria state that high-quality NGSS-aligned assessments should contain 0% DOK Level 1 (recall) tasks. On this assessment, an estimated 84% of items are at DOK1 (all 27 Scripted items). This is the heaviest recall concentration seen so far — nearly 5 out of every 6 questions require only memorized facts. With an average complexity of just 1.62, this is a very weak test that offers almost no opportunity for scientific reasoning.

What Do the Complexity Levels Mean?

From Achieve's Framework to Evaluate the Cognitive Complexity of Science Assessment Tasks (2019). Each question is classified based on how students are expected to think.

Scripted (Recall)
Student recalls a fact, definition, or formula. No reasoning required. Example: "What is the function of a root?" These are DOK Level 1 tasks. Achieve says NGSS-aligned tests should have 0% of these.
Low Guided (Application)
Student applies a known concept to a familiar scenario. Some reasoning, but the path is clear. Example: "A cart is heavy. Which action makes it easier to move?" Target: 40-50% of assessment.
High Guided (Transfer)
Student applies understanding to a new or unfamiliar situation. Must integrate multiple ideas and make sense of data or evidence. Example: "Given this data table, which claim about magnet strength is best supported?" Target: 20-30%.
Doing Science (Authentic)
Student engages in genuine scientific reasoning — analyzing novel data, constructing explanations, designing investigations. No single "right path." Example: "A population of mice changed color over 10 years. Use the evidence to explain why." Target: 5-10%.

Indicator Scores (Average Across All 32 Questions)

Achieve Cognitive Complexity Framework — Scale: 1 (Low) to 5 (High). Target for balanced assessment: ~3.0
What Are These Four Indicators?

Achieve's Cognitive Complexity Framework scores every assessment question on four dimensions. Together, these four scores determine the holistic classification (Scripted → Doing Science). A score of 1 means no engagement; 5 means deep, authentic engagement.

Scenario (Score: 1.6 avg)
Does the question present a phenomenon — something puzzling, observable, or real-world — that the student needs to figure out? A score of 1 means no context at all ("Define photosynthesis"). A score of 5 means a rich, novel scenario the student hasn't seen before.
SEP — Science & Engineering Practices (Score: 1.5 avg)
Does the question require the student to do something a scientist would do — analyze data, construct an explanation, argue from evidence, plan an investigation? A score of 1 means pure recall. A score of 5 means the student independently selects and applies a practice to solve a problem.
DCI — Disciplinary Core Ideas (Score: 2.1 avg)
How deeply does the question engage the science content? A score of 1 means recalling a definition. A score of 5 means applying core ideas to a new situation and connecting them across topics. For example: not just "What is energy?" but "Use this data to trace how energy transforms as it moves through a system."
CCC — Crosscutting Concepts (Score: 1.4 avg)
Does the question require the student to use a big-picture thinking lens like Cause & Effect, Patterns, Energy & Matter, or Stability & Change? These are the transferable thinking tools NGSS says students need across all science domains. A score of 1 means no CCC is engaged at all. This is G4.4's weakest area.

Content Coverage Flags

Issues with construct redundancy and topic balance
  • CRITICAL: Renewable/Nonrenewable Energy — 3 questions (Q2, Q4, Q22)
    Nearly 10% of the test asks the same recall question in different wording: "Which is renewable?" / "Which is nonrenewable?" / "Which keeps the atmosphere clean?" All three are Scripted with identical cognitive demand. Remove 2 of 3 and replace with questions that require students to evaluate energy trade-offs using data.
  • CRITICAL: Energy Classification Recall — 3 questions (Q12, Q13, Q28)
    Three questions test the same concept: classifying examples as "energy" or identifying the energy type of a simple action. All are Scripted at 1.0-1.25 complexity. Remove 2, keep one and redesign it to require tracing energy transformations through a system.
  • Duplicate: Energy Conversion — 2 questions (Q3, Q5)
    Q3 asks what electrical energy converts to in a light bulb; Q5 asks what happens when water is heated. Both are pure recall of energy conversion/phase change with no reasoning. Remove one.
  • Duplicate: Structure Function Recall — 3 questions (Q9, Q11, Q24)
    All three test trivial organism structure identification ("What do organisms' structures support?" / "What do gills do?" / "What allows pigs to find food?"). All are Scripted at 1.0-1.25. Remove 2 and redesign the remaining one with a specific organism scenario requiring Structure and Function reasoning.
  • Duplicate: ESS3-1 Pollution/Resources — 4 questions (Q2, Q4, Q6, Q22)
    Four questions are mapped to the same standard (4-ESS3-1) and all test at recall level. This single standard accounts for 13% of the test. Reduce to 1-2 questions and elevate cognitive demand by requiring data analysis.
  • Zero Engineering Questions
    No questions address Engineering, Technology, and Applications of Science (ETS). The G4 NGSS target is 18%. Use freed slots from redundancy removal to add ETS items requiring design evaluation and iterative testing.
  • No Questions Above Low Guided
    The highest complexity level on this test is Low Guided (5 questions at 2.0-3.0 avg). Zero questions reach High Guided or Doing Science. Achieve targets 25-40% of questions at these upper levels.

Achieve Assessment Criteria Summary

Criteria for Procuring and Evaluating High-Quality Science Assessments (2018)
What Do These Criteria Measure?

Achieve published 7 criteria for evaluating science assessments. The 4 most relevant to individual question quality are shown below. Each question is rated: Meets, Partially Meets, or Does Not Meet.

Criterion 2: Three-Dimensional
Does the question require students to use all three NGSS dimensions together — a Science/Engineering Practice (SEP), a Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and a Crosscutting Concept (CCC)? A question that only tests vocabulary is not three-dimensional.
Criterion 3: Phenomena / Problems
Is the question anchored in a real-world phenomenon (something observable that needs explaining) or an engineering problem? Students should be figuring out why or how something happens, not just recalling a definition.
Criterion 5: Cognitive Complexity
Does the question require real thinking? It should go beyond recall (DOK1) and ask students to analyze, evaluate, or construct explanations. Achieve's standard: an NGSS-aligned assessment should have 0% pure recall items.
Criterion 6: Technical Quality
Is the question well-constructed? Scientifically accurate content, clear wording, plausible distractors (wrong answers that reveal real misconceptions), no cueing or trick phrasing. A question can be technically sound but still too simple.

Technically Sound but Cognitively Empty

This test's technical quality is its only strength: 25 of 32 questions are well-constructed with accurate science and plausible distractors. But not a single question fully meets the three-dimensionality, phenomena, or complexity criteria. The fundamental problem is not question quality — it's that the questions measure vocabulary memorization rather than scientific reasoning. Students could score well by studying a glossary.

Achieve Standard: 0% DOK1 Items

Achieve's criteria state that high-quality NGSS-aligned assessments should contain 0% DOK Level 1 (recall) tasks. On this assessment, an estimated 42% of items are at DOK1 (all 13 Scripted items). While this is better than many Grade 3 assessments, the test still lacks upper-level complexity: only 2 questions (6%) reach High Guided, and none reach Doing Science. The assessment's CCC indicator (1.9/5) is critically low — crosscutting reasoning is almost never required.

What Do the Complexity Levels Mean?

From Achieve's Framework to Evaluate the Cognitive Complexity of Science Assessment Tasks (2019). Each question is classified based on how students are expected to think.

Scripted (Recall)
Student recalls a fact, definition, or formula. No reasoning required. Example: "What is the function of a root?" These are DOK Level 1 tasks. Achieve says NGSS-aligned tests should have 0% of these.
Low Guided (Application)
Student applies a known concept to a familiar scenario. Some reasoning, but the path is clear. Example: "A cart is heavy. Which action makes it easier to move?" Target: 40-50% of assessment.
High Guided (Transfer)
Student applies understanding to a new or unfamiliar situation. Must integrate multiple ideas and make sense of data or evidence. Example: "Given this data table, which claim about magnet strength is best supported?" Target: 20-30%.
Doing Science (Authentic)
Student engages in genuine scientific reasoning — analyzing novel data, constructing explanations, designing investigations. No single "right path." Example: "A population of mice changed color over 10 years. Use the evidence to explain why." Target: 5-10%.

Indicator Scores (Average Across All 31 Questions)

Achieve Cognitive Complexity Framework — Scale: 1 (Low) to 5 (High). Target for balanced assessment: ~3.0
What Are These Four Indicators?

Achieve's Cognitive Complexity Framework scores every assessment question on four dimensions. Together, these four scores determine the holistic classification (Scripted → Doing Science). A score of 1 means no engagement; 5 means deep, authentic engagement.

Scenario (Score: 2.5 avg)
Does the question present a phenomenon — something puzzling, observable, or real-world — that the student needs to figure out? A score of 1 means no context at all ("Define photosynthesis"). A score of 5 means a rich, novel scenario the student hasn't seen before.
SEP — Science & Engineering Practices (Score: 2.3 avg)
Does the question require the student to do something a scientist would do — analyze data, construct an explanation, argue from evidence, plan an investigation? A score of 1 means pure recall. A score of 5 means the student independently selects and applies a practice to solve a problem.
DCI — Disciplinary Core Ideas (Score: 2.6 avg)
How deeply does the question engage the science content? A score of 1 means recalling a definition. A score of 5 means applying core ideas to a new situation and connecting them across topics. For example: not just "What is energy?" but "Use this data to explain how energy is transferred from the sun to warming water."
CCC — Crosscutting Concepts (Score: 1.9 avg)
Does the question require the student to use a big-picture thinking lens like Cause & Effect, Patterns, Structure & Function, Systems, or Scale? These are the transferable thinking tools NGSS says students need across all science domains. A score of 1 means no CCC is engaged at all. This is G4.1's weakest area.

Content Coverage Flags

Issues with construct redundancy, topic balance, and off-grade content
  • CRITICAL: Rock Layers / Superposition (4-ESS1-1) — 6 questions (Q1, Q3, Q5a/b, Q6, Q10, Q21)
    Nearly 20% of the test assesses the same standard at similar complexity levels. Q1, Q3, Q5a, Q6, Q10, and Q21 all ask students to order rock layers or determine fossil ages using superposition. Q5b is the only one that demands evidence-based argumentation. Remove 3–4 of these and replace with under-represented standards (ETS, other PS/LS).
  • Redundancy: Energy Forms / PS3-2 — 7 questions (Q7, Q9, Q15, Q24, Q25, Q27, Q28)
    Seven questions test energy identification or transfer, many at Scripted recall level ("Which energy form?" or "Which direction does heat flow?"). Q9, Q15, Q24, Q25, Q27, and Q28 are all Scripted. Reduce to 3–4 diverse energy questions with genuine reasoning.
  • Severe ETS Underrepresentation
    Only 1 ETS question (Q26) out of 31. NGSS targets 18% for Engineering & Technology. Q26 is also the test's strongest item (High Guided, 3.25 avg). Use freed slots from redundancy removal to add 4–5 more ETS items at this quality level.
  • Life Science Consists Entirely of Recall
    All 5 LS questions (Q8, Q12, Q13, Q17, Q18) are Scripted. Three are pure recall with no scenario (Q8: "which sense?", Q17: "which flower parts?", Q18: "which two organs?"). Redesign LS items with phenomena and evidence-based reasoning.
  • No Doing Science Questions
    The highest complexity level reached is High Guided (Q5b, Q26). Zero questions reach Doing Science. Achieve targets 5–10% of questions at this level. The test misses the opportunity for authentic scientific reasoning.
  • Q26 Correct Answer Concern
    Q26 lists "Block doors to stop water" as the correct answer (C), but "Raise community center floor above water level" (B) is arguably a more effective engineering solution that also meets both constraints. Review answer key for accuracy.

Achieve Assessment Criteria Summary

Criteria for Procuring and Evaluating High-Quality Science Assessments (2018)
What Do These Criteria Measure?

Achieve published 7 criteria for evaluating science assessments. The 4 most relevant to individual question quality are shown below. Each question is rated: Meets, Partially Meets, or Does Not Meet.

Criterion 2: Three-Dimensional
Does the question require students to use all three NGSS dimensions together — a Science/Engineering Practice (SEP), a Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and a Crosscutting Concept (CCC)? A question that only tests vocabulary is not three-dimensional.
Criterion 3: Phenomena / Problems
Is the question anchored in a real-world phenomenon (something observable that needs explaining) or an engineering problem? Students should be figuring out why or how something happens, not just recalling a definition.
Criterion 5: Cognitive Complexity
Does the question require real thinking? It should go beyond recall (DOK1) and ask students to analyze, evaluate, or construct explanations. Achieve's standard: an NGSS-aligned assessment should have 0% pure recall items.
Criterion 6: Technical Quality
Is the question well-constructed? Scientifically accurate content, clear wording, plausible distractors (wrong answers that reveal real misconceptions), no cueing or trick phrasing. A question can be technically sound but still too simple.

Technically Sound but Cognitively Shallow

84% of questions meet or partially meet technical quality standards — they have accurate science, clear wording, and plausible distractors. However, zero questions fully meet criteria for three-dimensional design, phenomena grounding, or cognitive complexity. The fundamental issue is not question construction but cognitive demand: students are asked to recall and identify rather than reason, analyze, and explain.

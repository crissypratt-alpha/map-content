Achieve Standard: 0% DOK1 Items

Achieve's criteria state that high-quality NGSS-aligned assessments should contain 0% DOK Level 1 (recall) tasks. On this assessment, an estimated 36% of items are at DOK1 (8 Scripted items). While still above target, this is significantly better than G3.1 (60%) and G3.12 (60%).

What Do the Complexity Levels Mean?

From Achieve's Framework to Evaluate the Cognitive Complexity of Science Assessment Tasks (2019). Each question is classified based on how students are expected to think.

Scripted (Recall)
Student recalls a fact, definition, or formula. No reasoning required. These are DOK Level 1 tasks. Achieve says NGSS-aligned tests should have 0% of these.
Low Guided (Application)
Student applies a known concept to a familiar scenario. Some reasoning, but the path is clear. Target: 40-50% of assessment.
High Guided (Transfer)
Student applies understanding to a new or unfamiliar situation. Must integrate multiple ideas and make sense of data or evidence. Target: 20-30%.
Doing Science (Authentic)
Student engages in genuine scientific reasoning — analyzing novel data, constructing explanations, designing investigations. Target: 5-10%.

What Are These Four Indicators?

Achieve's Cognitive Complexity Framework scores every assessment question on four dimensions. Together, these four scores determine the holistic classification (Scripted → Doing Science). A score of 1 means no engagement; 5 means deep, authentic engagement.

Scenario (Score: 2.3 avg)
Does the question present a phenomenon — something puzzling, observable, or real-world — that the student needs to figure out?
SEP — Science & Engineering Practices (Score: 2.0 avg)
Does the question require the student to do something a scientist would do — analyze data, construct an explanation, argue from evidence?
DCI — Disciplinary Core Ideas (Score: 2.4 avg)
How deeply does the question engage the science content? Goes from recalling a definition (1) to applying core ideas to new situations (5).
CCC — Crosscutting Concepts (Score: 2.0 avg)
Does the question require thinking with lenses like Cause & Effect, Patterns, Structure & Function? This remains the weakest area across all G3 tests.

Content Coverage Flags

Issues with construct redundancy and topic balance
  • Life Cycle Sequence Recall — 3 questions (Q1, Q2, Q3)
    Three questions testing life cycle knowledge at varying complexity. Q1 and Q3 are pure sequence recall. Keep Q2 (comparative reasoning), redesign or remove Q1 and Q3.
  • Adaptation/Habitat — 2 questions (Q9, Q10)
    Both test organism-environment matching at low complexity. Q10 is too easy (penguin in desert). Keep Q9 as a foundation, replace Q10 with a data-driven adaptation question.
  • No Doing Science Questions
    The test's highest complexity level is High Guided (3 questions = 14%). While this is the best G3 test yet, it still lacks authentic science reasoning at the Doing Science level. Target: 5-10%.

Bright Spots: Best Domain Balance of Any G3 Test

G3.13 is the first G3 test with near-ideal domain balance (LS 45%, PS 27%, ESS 14%, ETS 14% — all within 5% of NGSS targets). The data-driven weather questions (Q11, Q12) and engineering design question (Q13) are strong models for other tests.

Priority 1: Eliminate Recall Questions (Free 4 Slots)
1
Remove pure sequence recall (Q1, Q3) and obvious habitat matching (Q10)
Q1 (butterfly stage recall) and Q3 (bean plant sequence) test memorization only. Q10 (penguin in desert) is too obvious. Remove these 3 and replace with higher-complexity items. Also remove Q14 (pull rope toward yourself — answers itself).
Frees 4 question slots
Priority 2: Elevate Existing Questions to Higher Complexity
2
Push Q7 (penguin huddle) and Q8 (seashell fossil) to High Guided
Both questions already have good phenomena. Add a follow-up question requiring students to explain the mechanism or evaluate competing claims. Q7: "Why do the penguins take turns moving to the center?" Q8: "What additional evidence would confirm the area was underwater?"

3
Add "Doing Science" level by extending the weather data questions
Q11 and Q12 are already strong. Add a Part B that asks students to compare Perth and Kansas City climate patterns and explain why they differ (Southern vs. Northern Hemisphere), requiring students to construct an explanation from cross-data-set analysis.
Creates Doing Science items from existing strengths
Priority 3: Strengthen Weakest Questions
4
Add data to charge interaction (Q17) and adaptation (Q9)
Q17 tests charge rules as pure recall. Provide experimental data showing what happens when different charged objects are brought together and ask students to identify the pattern. Q9: provide data about Arctic animals' body measurements and ask students to find the pattern linking body features to cold survival.
